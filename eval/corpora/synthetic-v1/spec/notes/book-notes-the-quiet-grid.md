# The Quiet Grid — notes

Pop-science tour of how electricity grids actually balance, written for
the "I pay the bill and vaguely worry" audience, which is me.

The keepers:

- Grid frequency as the universal truth signal — everything on the grid
  agrees on one number, and that number IS supply-vs-demand. Lovely
  framing. The whole system is a distributed consensus protocol with
  physics as the network layer (my gloss, book doesn't say it like that)
- Peak shaving beats peak generating: the cheapest power station is the
  kettle you didn't switch on. Chapter on demand response made
  time-of-use tariffs finally click
- Heat pumps chapter directly relevant to [[heat-pump-research]]: the
  author's point that a heat pump at SCOP 4 turns 1 kWh of electricity
  into 4 kWh of heat means the *grid* impact of electrified heating is a
  quarter of the naive maths. Aurellon's quote claims SCOP 4.1, which
  the book would call "plausible for a well-designed retrofit, audit the
  design temperature assumption" — theirs is 5.8 kW at −2°C, noted
- Storage economics: batteries earn on volatility not volume →
  [[article-notes-home-batteries]] follows this thread

Quibbles: UK-centric (fine for me now, would've annoyed Brooklyn me),
and the interconnector chapter aged badly per the afterword's own
admission.

Verdict 4/5, the rare pop-science book with working numbers in the back.

# Home batteries — do they make sense for the flat? (spoiler: no)

Follow-on thread from [[book-notes-the-quiet-grid]] and the heat pump
thing. Read a stack of articles + two long forum threads. Summary before
I forget why I concluded what I concluded:

The case FOR, in general: charge cheap overnight on a time-of-use
tariff, discharge at peak, arbitrage the spread. With an EV or solar the
maths compounds. Payback horizons people claim: 7-12 years, battery
warranty typically 10.

The case AGAINST, for me specifically:

1. I'm on Fair Deal Flex at a flat 25.3p/kWh — no time-of-use spread to
   arbitrage. Would need to switch tariff first, and Harwood's TOU
   offering needs the half-hourly data sharing enabled
   ([[smart-meter-data]] gap: my meter went silent for 3 weeks in Feb,
   inspiring confidence)
2. Usage is 3,043 kWh/yr ([[energy-costs-first-year]]) — small. The
   spread on that, even generously, is £80-120/yr. A battery install is
   £4-5k. The arithmetic laughs at me
3. It's a rented second-floor flat. No solar, no garage, and Wrenmoor
   barely just agreed to *think* about a heat pump
   ([[heat-pump-research]]). A wall of lithium is not making it past
   Sophie's email filter
4. If the heat pump happens, usage roughly doubles → spread value maybe
   £200/yr. Still a decade-plus payback. Still no.

Verdict: file under "own a house first". Revisit trigger: TOU spread
>15p AND owning walls.

One genuinely interesting tangent: flat-friendly "balcony solar" plug-in
panels are apparently normal in parts of Europe and basically absent
here — regulatory, not technical. Would cover the homelab's 14W baseline
draw ([[homelab-inventory]]) about 40x over. Keep an eye on that.
